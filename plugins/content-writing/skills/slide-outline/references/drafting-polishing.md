## 1. The 6 Principles of a Great Slide

Think of each slide as a billboard — the audience has seconds to absorb it.

| Principle | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Clear** | One key message clearly conveyed. Concise and clear language throughout. |
| **Simple** | One message per slide. No clutter. |
| **Powerful** | Replace text with pictures, charts, or diagrams that link the key message to visuals. |
| **Coherent** | Transparent logic — obvious and consistent connections between data and conclusions. |
| **Honest** | Provide the right context for the data. Market share cannot be conveyed via absolute revenue. Use the visual that accurately represents the claim. |
| **Audience-centric** | The intersection of "what you want to say" and "what they're interested in." Every decision filters through: *what does this audience need?* |

**Two self-test questions:**
1. Was my one key message clearly conveyed?
2. Did I use concise and clear language on the slide?

## 2. Slide Elements

A well-structured slide has up to four elements:

### Title
- Provides the **main takeaway** (implications, not description)
- Every slide must have one

### Body
- Provides **proof** of the title message
- Contains clear and concise content with logical flow
- Use matching words between title and body to minimize work for the reader
- Guide the viewer's eye to the data that supports your title
- Build consistent logic: reading only the main points of the body should support the title (Pyramid Principle)

### Subtitle *(optional — Detail slides only)*
**Correct uses:**
- Serves as a roadmap for the body
- Guides the audience to more specific information
- Helps the audience interpret the slide
- Adds useful detail to the title

**Incorrect uses — avoid these:**
- Using on Key Message (Normal) slides
- Using when title is already two lines
- Adding another message/point
- Using as extra space to complete the title
- Qualifying or polishing the title

### Footer / Citation *(optional)*
- Use on slides with data, quotes, or analysis
- Content must be sufficient to find the source document
- If the same data is cited elsewhere in the deck, it doesn't need to be re-cited
- "BCG analysis" should note the insight was developed via analytics, not from a specific source

## 3. Effective Titles

An effective title has **four characteristics**:

1. **Highlights the key message** and matches the body of the slide
2. **Is concise and specific** — use numbers over vague qualifiers
3. **Provides the "so-what" / actionable next step** — recommendation or implication, not description
4. **Links the story together** — connects to the previous and next slide's narrative

### Generic vs. Insightful Titles

| Ineffective | Improved | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "Observations on the relationship between hotel price and business travel" | "Price erosion of hotels likely results from slowing of business travel and reduced T&E budget" | Concise and specific |
| "Of seven options analyzed, offshore manufacturing shows the most potential" | "Offshore manufacturing can improve margins by 20%" | Focus on key option, specific upside |
| "Administration cost has risen significantly compared with 2013" | "Administration cost has tripled in two years" | Specific ("tripled" vs "risen significantly") |
| "Six product categories generate most sales; the remaining 20 contribute 20%" | "Recommend we focus on the six product categories that generate 80% sales" | Adds recommendation (so-what) |
| "Key enablers for the new business model" | "The new business model requires new HR capabilities, tracking tools, and performance KPIs" | Action-oriented, specific |

### Titles tell a story
A set of descriptive titles reads as a table of contents. A set of insightful titles reads as a narrative. Test: can you read just the titles and understand the full argument?

## 4. Body Design

### Body Objectives & Techniques

| Objective | Technique |
|---|---|
| **Minimize work** for the reader | Use matching words between title and body; maintain the same order as mentioned in the title |
| **Guide viewer's eye** to key data | Use color, size, position, and call-out boxes to highlight critical numbers |
| **Build consistent logic** | Apply Pyramid Principle — reading only the main body points should support the title |

### Key/Detail Slide Structure
Each Key Message slide and its supporting Detail slides should form **one pyramid** — MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) at both the key and detail level.

### Visual Types

**Charts** — Use when demonstrating quantitative data relationships:

| Chart Type | Best For |
|---|---|
| Area chart | Cumulated totals over time (use stacked column if <10 data points) |
| Stacked column | Same as area, but dimension need not be time |
| Waterfall | Cumulative effect of sequentially introduced positive/negative values |
| Donut chart | Product mix, market segmentation, questionnaire responses |
| Line chart | Compare across time (use column if <10 data points) |
| Column chart | Compare across players/markets/time |
| Bar chart | When you need longer y-axis; don't use for time |
| Bubble chart | 3 or 4 dimensions; also used for portfolio analysis |
| Histogram / Bell curve | Proportion of cases within an interval |
| Map with bubbles | Geographic data |
| Variwide | 2–3 dimensional stacked chart; most effective when largest segment is bottom-left |
| Spider / Radar chart | Gap analysis, e.g., perception vs. reality |
| XY scatter plot | Regressions or relationships between two variables |
| Correlation bar chart | Two players across attributes, or two attributes across players |

**Tables** — Prefer over graphs when:
- Large amount of qualitative or quantitative information
- Precise values are crucial
- Comparison or calculations need to be shown
- Multiple units of measure involved

**Diagrams** — Use when:
- Representing large amounts of information efficiently
- Showing cause and effect
- Depicting sequence and flow
- Making content visually interesting and easier to remember

Diagram types: Process flow, Organization chart, Planning/Gantt, Tree, Harvey balls, Matrix, Map, Conceptual

## 5. Slide Language (DDR Process)

### The DDR Framework

```
Draft  →  Get your story down on paper
  ↓
Drain  →  Take out unnecessary words (make it Concise)
  ↓
Refine →  Make your language more effective (other 4 principles)
```

### 5 Language Principles

#### Concise (Drain step)
Be succinct. Cut every word that doesn't earn its place.

| Wordy | More Succinct |
|---|---|
| "It is absolutely crucial to change multiple dimensions of our salesforce team" | "Imperative that we transform salesforce" |
| "There is likely to be a substantial economic prize of $1 billion resulting from taking this action" | "This action can add $1 billion in EBIT" |
| "Suppliers who are accustomed to the old system must be supported" | "Imperative that we support suppliers using old system" |

#### Consistent
- Maintain **parallel structure** — bullet points should all be verbs or all be nouns, not mixed
- Same tense throughout
- Same verb forms across bullets

#### Clear
- Replace vague qualifiers with specific numbers: "significant improvement" → "50% to 62%"
- Avoid jargon and internal acronyms — even for internal teams
- Specific beats vague every time

#### Punchy
- Use **active voice**: "Company A just launched its new phone" beats "A new phone was just launched by Company A"
- Express actions as **verbs, not nouns**: "Understanding the underlying reasons for change will be crucial in designing the new processes" beats "An understanding of the underlying reasons for change will be crucial to the design of the new processes"
- Be **direct and confident**: "Recommend purchasing service" beats "Might consider buying at least part of the service"

#### Purposeful
- Focus on the **main characters** of the story (the subjects of your sentences)
- Choose words and tone according to audience and occasion
- Use inclusive language to avoid sensitivities
- Use thought-provoking / engaging language for discussion contexts

**Example of purposeful framing:**

| Claim | Sample situation |
|---|---|
| "Company X is losing customers to Company Y" | Present to senior clients of Company X; use as a wake-up call |
| "Company Y is taking customers from Company X" | Focus on a competitor; minimize risk of blaming a specific audience member |
| "Customers are switching from Company X to Company Y" | Maintain neutral focus (e.g., a proposal, a sensitive situation) |

### Full DDR Example

**Before (wordy/weak):**
> *It is possible that Possible issues facing the firm at this point in time are:*
> - *Our Unable to retain the best people at the time in which when they are likely to add most value to the firm most valuable*
> - *Failure to be able to attract fresh talent from top schools (Number of new recruits from top schools down significantly last year)*

**After (Drained + Refined):**
> **Our clear priority is talent**
> - Retain the best and most valuable talent
> - Attract fresh talent from top schools (dropped around 20% last year)

*Changes applied: Punchy (confident title), Purposeful ("our"), Consistent (aligned verb forms), Punchy (active voice in bullets), Clear (specific % instead of "significantly")*

## 6. Key Questions for Self-Testing Each Slide

| Element | Questions to Ask |
|---|---|
| **Title** | Does it express the main point? Can you sense the story from just the titles? Does it provide the "so what"? |
| **Slide body** | Does it support the main point/title? Is the appropriate graphic used? Is data displayed clearly without unnecessary information? Is the layout easy to follow? |
| **Subtitle** | Does it guide the reader to what is most important? Does it predict key concepts from the body? Does it tell the reader how to process the slide? |
| **Footnotes/Citation** | Is the citation sufficient to find the source? Will explanatory information in the footnote make the slide complete? |
